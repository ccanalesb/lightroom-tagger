# Environmental Context Legibility

Whether the street environment around the subject stays sharp and readable enough to show place, spatial relationships, and parallel activity—not reduced to undifferentiated blur. This scores the *visible outcome* of depth of field (what the viewer can read), not the aperture setting used to make the image.

**Source anchor:** The author argues that using an extremely open aperture "makes very little sense" because it fails to show the street and the interaction of things.

## Scoring (1-10)

- **weak** (1-3): The subject is visually isolated by shallow depth of field; background and midground collapse into color fields or unrecognizable shapes. The viewer cannot identify the type of place (shopfront, crossing, market aisle), count secondary figures, or see how the subject relates to surrounding activity. The image could almost be a studio portrait dropped onto a generic blur.
- **adequate** (4-6): Some environmental context is visible—general street or interior readable—but important midground or background detail is soft, cropped away, or too dark to parse. Place is identifiable in broad terms (e.g. "a city sidewalk") but specific relationships, parallel events, or architectural cues that connect subject to environment are only partially legible.
- **strong** (7-10): Subject and surrounding street (or public interior) are both clearly readable at useful detail: signage, architecture, other pedestrians, vehicles, or spatial depth remain sharp enough to show *interaction*—what else is happening near the subject and where they sit in the scene. Even selective focus keeps enough context that the photograph shows the street, not just a figure cut out from it.
